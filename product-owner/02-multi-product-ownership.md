## Running multiple products at different stages at the same time

> **In one line:** I owned product decisions across four active efforts at once — a mature product, a second mature product, a brand-new product, and a client integration on a hard deadline — without letting any of them go quiet.

**Situation.** I was responsible for product decisions on four parallel efforts: a mature SaaS product with an active e-commerce release and real technical debt, a second mature product, a brand-new product being built from scratch, and a client-facing integration with an external marketplace partner on a fixed deadline. Each one needed a different working rhythm.

**What I did.**
- For the mature product: tracked and prioritized high-priority bugs per engineer, kept a clear, up-to-date view of ownership and overdue work, and coordinated a focused close-out before a release deadline.
- For the new product: ran a structured, epic-by-epic scoping process with its own pace (see the [zero-to-one case study](./01-zero-to-one-launch.md)).
- For the partner integration: managed it as a checklist of external dependencies, each with a named owner, and followed up individually — a single coordinated handoff meeting was not realistic given the partner's timeline.
- Applied different cadences on purpose: strict capacity planning and sprint hygiene for the mature product, dependency-based epics for the new product, and daily follow-up for the partner integration.

**Result.** All four efforts kept moving forward, with clear stakeholder alignment across teams and no single one silently stalling because it was not the current focus. Each product got the operating rhythm it actually needed, instead of one-size-fits-all process.

**Skills:** cross-functional collaboration, prioritization under competing deadlines, stakeholder management, scalable process design.
