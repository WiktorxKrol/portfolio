## Scoping and launching a new product from zero

`⏱️ ~2 min read`

> **🎯 Result:** A 70-task, ~570-hour backlog engineering could actually build — scoped from a mockup and a strategy doc, with the rollout paced enough to survive pressure to rush it.

<details>
<summary><b>🧩 Situation</b> — a SaaS product that existed only as a mockup, with pressure to build it all at once</summary>

A new SaaS product (property rental management, for landlords, tenants, and property managers) existed only as a design mockup and a strategy document. Nothing had been scoped or estimated. Leadership wanted the whole product built as fast as possible, including pressure to plan and build everything in one push.

</details>

<details>
<summary><b>⚙️ What I did</b> — 5 steps, from full scoping to legal triage</summary>

- Took full, end-to-end ownership of the scoping process: read every screen and every section of the strategy document, and converted them into about 70 scoped and estimated tasks (roughly 570 hours of work), covering business analysis, design, and engineering.
- Organized the backlog into 12 epics (authentication, contract generation, payments, tenant panel, marketplace, and more), sequenced by dependency, so infrastructure and login were built before anything that needed them.
- Used a deliberate, paced rollout instead of dumping the entire backlog into the tracker at once — one epic scoped and reviewed per session, with a clear checkpoint each time, to keep stakeholder alignment throughout the process.
- Made and documented clear scope decisions along the way. For example, I removed a "nice to have" AI dashboard assistant from the MVP, but kept simpler lifecycle emails (onboarding reminders, re-engagement) because they had a direct, measurable link to user activation.
- Flagged legal and compliance questions (contract validity, deposit limits, e-signature requirements) explicitly, and routed them to outside legal counsel instead of letting engineering assume the answers.

</details>

**📈 Result.** A backlog that stakeholders could review and adjust step by step, instead of a wall of unreviewed scope. The team's delivery pace stayed realistic and sustainable, even with pressure to move faster than the process could support.

🏷️ `Requirement Scoping` `Backlog Structuring` `Stakeholder Management` `Dependency-Based Planning` `Scope Prioritization`
