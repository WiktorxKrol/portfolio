# Scoping and launching a new product from zero

**Context:** A new SaaS product (property rental management, for landlords/tenants/property managers) at the concept stage — a design mockup and a strategy document, nothing else built or scoped.

**Problem:** Someone had to turn "here's a mockup and a doc" into a backlog engineering could actually start building against, under real pressure from leadership to move as fast as possible — including pressure to have the whole thing scoped and built in one push.

**What I did:**
- Read every screen in the mockup and every section of the strategy doc, and translated them into ~70 scoped, estimated work items (~570 hours total) across business analysis, design, and engineering.
- Organized the backlog into 12 epics (auth, contract generation, payments/deposits, tenant panel, marketplace, etc.), sequenced by dependency — infrastructure and auth before anything that needs them.
- Deliberately paced the rollout: one epic scoped and reviewed per work session, each with an explicit checkpoint, instead of dumping the full backlog into the tracker at once.
- Made and documented explicit scope calls along the way — for example, cutting a "nice to have" AI dashboard assistant from MVP entirely, while keeping the less flashy lifecycle emails (onboarding nudge, re-engagement) in scope because they map directly to activation.
- Flagged legal/compliance open questions explicitly (contract validity, deposit limits, e-signature) and routed them to outside counsel instead of letting engineering assume answers.

**Outcome:** A backlog that could be reviewed and adjusted incrementally instead of a wall of untested scope. The team's actual working pace stayed sustainable despite pressure to move faster than the process could absorb.

**Skills:** scope sequencing, stakeholder pacing, saying no to speed for its own sake, dependency-ordered epic planning.
